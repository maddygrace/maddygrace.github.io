a little about me

Hiya, I'm Maddy and I'm a first year PhD Student at the University of Birmingham, UK.
My main area of research is planets around stars much smaller than the Sun, i.e., M dwarfs, specifically those with effective temperatures less than 3300K. I primarily use data from the NASA TESS Space Telescope and the ground-based SPECULOOS telescopes in Paranal, Chile. Click here to read more about my research.

my research

I study exoplanets that orbit late-type M dwarfs, typically with effective temperatures < 3300K. The goal of my research is to determine occurrence rates of exoplanets around these stars; I aim to do this by trying to detect additional planets in systems which already have one known planet. Planet formation theories, as well as our own Solar System, suggest that planets likely do not form in isolation. Does this extend to cool stars?
By selecting stars which are likely to have detectable, transiting planets, as well as assessing our detection limits for photometric data (code currently in progress!), this is a question I hope to answer during my PhD.
The SPECULOOS telescopes at the Paranal Observatory in Chile offer a chance to obtain additonal, ground-based follow-up photometric data. The advantage of these telescopes is that they obtain better precision on a single transit for cool M dwarfs compared to the NASA TESS Space Telescope, allowing us to probe a parameter space of planets with smaller radii. Furthermore, stars not in or near the continuous viewing zone (CVZ) of TESS are often limited by the 27-day sector lengths, potentially preventing detections of long-period exoplanets. With the goal of 100 hours of continuous follow-up per target, we also aim to probe the parameter space of longer-period planets.
Please don't hesitate to get in touch if you'd like to hear more about my work!
